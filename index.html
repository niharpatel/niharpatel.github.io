Data Driven Differential Diagnosis

A Massive Challenge

Dementia is a chronic, progressive, and debilitating disease. Nearly 15% of individuals older than 71 suffer from some form of dementia. Despite its severity and widespread prevalence, little progress has been made in its prevention or treatment. Research has helped us better understand the mechanics of the disease, but clinical trials have shown limited success.

Difficult Diagnoses

Because dementia is an assortment of distinct processes with similar symptoms, an individual diagnosed with dementia could be suffering from any of multiple diseases, and could be in any stage of their particular disease. This makes it difficult to determine the exact dementia subtype. The challenge of diagnosis contributes to the challenge of effective treatments; the correct medicine for one disease and stage of illness could be different from the correct medicine for another.

Challenging Long-Term Care

While cancer and heart disease appropriately receive a great deal of attention, the annual cost of treating dementia exceeds both these diseases. Dementia care cost the United States $259 billion annually. The high burden comes from both the prevalence of the disease and the high needs of these patients. The burden often falls on family members who are tasked with taking care of an adult loved one who cannot take care of basic needs for themselves. By 2040, as the population ages and the majority of the baby boomers enter the high risk period for dementia, these costs could double. We can already see rising rates of dementia even while other major diseases fall. Barring a breakthrough, the healthcare system is ill prepared to deal with the increased cost of caring for these patients.

Using Data to Deliver Better Outcomes

The National Alzheimer's Coordinating Center (NACC) has developed a standardized survey that is administered to patients entering one of their centers by specially trained staff members. The survey elciits information directly from the patient, from staff members with the opportunity to test the patient, from family memebers who live with the patient, and from a physician who performs a standardized history and physical exam. In total, over 750 different measures are collected for a given patient. However, diagnosis of the disease still requires neurologist specialists who must weigh the evidence for and against a given dementia subtype to provide the final diagnosis.

Identifying Early and Accurately

We used the NAAC dataset to build a machine learning classification model to help primary care physicians diagnose their patients using standardized assessments. To prove this use case, we focused on two more prevalent subtypes of dementia: Alzheimer's Disease and Behavioral Varient Frontotemporal Dementia. We created an open API from which healthcare professionals can upload a standardized patient survey and receive the predicted classification.

The Model

Our Goal

We set out to distinguish between Alzheimer's Disease (AD) and Behavioral Varient Frontotemporal Dementia (bvFTD) based on the data collected by the NACC:

Alzheimer's
Disease

  • Symptoms usually develop slowly and get worse over time, becoming severe enough to interfere with daily tasks.

  • 14,623 unique patients with 45,441 records.

Behavioral Varient Frontotemporal Dementia

  • Behavioral Variant FTD is characterized by prominent changes in personality, interpersonal relationships, and conduct.

  • This often occurs in younger patients compared to Alzheimer's disease.

  • 889 unique patients with 2,114 records.

Training

When approaching this problem, we realized that the way differential diagnoses are done for dementia patients are very distinct due to the multiple points of contact during the diagnosis process. With NACC's standardized surveys, whe had over 750 variables to choose from!

Thanks to the expert knowledge of one of our team members, we were able to assess and eliminate some non-explanatory data. But this left us with a challenge of how to go about deciding the importance of the rest of our features. To do this, we turned to machine learning.

Machine learning is a field of statistics that uses computer science and mathematical methods to analysis large data sets. Unlike humans, these statistical methods are very attuned to fine changes in the data, allowing them to pick up variations that are very detailed even when there is a lot of variation in the data.

For example, because AD and bvFTD exhibit different incidence rates at diffferent ages, we can use this as one variable in our model.

The model can then loop through all the other possible variables to determine which ones have the largest impact on our prediction. The process looks much like constructing a tree, a sample of which is detailed below:


At the bottom of this tree are the leafs, which is where a decision is actually made as to which type of dementia we would classify a given case. Trees can go for several hundred steps over hundreds or thousands of variables before a decision is actually made! This makes this method very well suited for complicated data sets. We actually used a random forest model -- which is an emsemble method of decision trees with nearly 1000 branches, which made our model highly accurate while still providing the sensitivity we needed to identify particular cases.


Once we had an idea of what our data looked like, we divided our data set into a training and test set. The training set was composed of about 80% of our data, while the test set was the remaining 20%. We then developed our model on the training set, and tried to use this model to predict the remaining 20%. This process allows us to ensure our results will generalize to other cases and we haven't overtrained our model to our training set. We trained a variety of models to test different types of models against each other to see which one gave the best results when we validated them.

Validation

To validate that our model was the best one possible, we used the ROC curve to understand how good our model compared with other models. A highly valid model will increase sharply then flatten out quickly. The result is a larger area under the curve, which we can then compare between different models. The best model will have the highest area under the curve, meaning it is more accurate than comparable models. We've shown the ROC curve for our final model below:
